The Global Korea Scholarship application has more moving parts than almost any other government scholarship. There are six forms to write, ten kinds of certificates to gather (depending on your situation), an apostille step that takes weeks, and three rounds of selection that span four months.

This guide is the operational checklist: every date, every document, every cutoff. The dates and form numbers come from the most recent NIIED guideline (2025 cycle); the 2026 cycle is expected to follow the same structure with shifted dates.

## The full GKS-U timeline

| Stage | Embassy Track | University Track |
|---|---|---|
| Application guidelines published (Study in Korea website) | September | September |
| First-round application deadline | Set by each embassy (typically late September – early October) | After **October 25** (set by each university) |
| Round 1 document examination result | By **October 11** | Set by each university |
| Round 1 oral interview result | By **October 18** | By **November 15** |
| Survivor docs arrive at NIIED | By **November 1** | By **November 15** |
| Round 2 (NIIED) result | By **late November** | By **mid-December** |
| Round 3 (universities) result | By **December 20** | — |
| Final university choice notification | By **December 27** | — |
| Final announcement of scholars | **Early January** | **Early January** |

<Callout variant="warn" title="watch the embassy deadline">
The Embassy Track deadline is set by **each embassy**, not by NIIED. Some embassies close applications in mid-September; others run into early October. Check your local embassy's website by mid-September. The University Track deadline is uniform: applications close after October 25 at every participating university, but the specific date varies by university.
</Callout>

## What you actually have to write: Forms 1–6

Every GKS-U application includes the same six forms, regardless of track. They are the heart of the application and where most evaluation points are won or lost.

### Form 1 — Application Form

The basic biographical document. Name (must match passport exactly, including middle name), citizenship, contact info, education history, language proficiency, and the universities/departments you're applying to.

**Common mistakes:** mismatched names (passport vs application), incorrect official department names (universities have very specific naming — copy-paste from the University Information file, don't guess), and listing three Type A universities on the Embassy Track when at least one must be Type B.

### Form 2 — Personal Statement

A 2–3 page essay covering your background, motivation, growth, and reasons for choosing Korea. NIIED weights this heavily.

The strong personal statements have:

- A specific origin story — *not* "I have always loved Korea since middle school."
- A through-line connecting your background to your intended major.
- One or two concrete moments of growth (challenge → response → outcome).
- A clear bridge between your past and your Korean education.

The weak ones are generic ("Korea is a leader in technology and culture, which is why I want to study there"), list achievements without context, or focus on Korean media instead of Korean academics.

### Form 3 — Study Plan

A 2–3 page document outlining what you'll do during the GKS scholarship: language year, semester-by-semester degree plan, research interests, post-graduation goals.

The strongest study plans answer four questions in concrete detail:

1. **Why this specific major and department?** Reference faculty, specific courses, the curriculum.
2. **Why this specific university?** Reference programs, labs, partnerships, geographic fit.
3. **Semester-by-semester plan** — what courses or research areas will you tackle in years 1, 2, 3, 4?
4. **Post-graduation plan** — how does this degree fit into a 5–10 year career arc, and how does Korea fit into that?

The study plan is also where you signal Korean-language commitment ("I will reach TOPIK Level 4 by the end of the language year by..."), research interests with specific faculty references, and post-graduation goals that justify the investment.

### Form 4 — Letter of Recommendation

**Exactly one** recommendation letter (not two, not three). Choose someone who knows you academically — a teacher, principal, or professor. The letter should speak to specific examples of your academic performance, character, and potential, and explicitly endorse you for GKS.

NIIED does **not** require the recommender to submit confidentially or by sealed envelope (each university may have its own rule), but the letter must be signed and on letterhead.

### Form 5 — GKS Applicant Agreement

A signed agreement acknowledging the rules of the scholarship: you'll commit to the program, abide by NIIED's regulations, return to your home country after graduation if required, etc. Read it carefully and sign with your handwritten signature in the original document.

### Form 6 — Personal Medical Assessment

A self-reported health questionnaire. Pre-existing conditions are not automatic disqualifiers, but conditions that would prevent you from completing 5–7 years of full-time study in Korea are. After arrival, all GKS scholars also undergo a formal medical examination.

## Required certificates

In addition to Forms 1–6, you'll submit:

- **Proof of citizenship** (yours and your parents') and proof of family relationship — typically a family register or birth certificate.
- **High school graduation certificate** (or certificate of expected graduation if you haven't graduated yet).
- **High school transcript** showing your CGPA in one of the recognized scales (4.0, 4.3, 4.5, 5.0, or 100-point) — or a converted, school-authenticated version if your scale isn't standard.
- **Associate degree graduation certificate and transcript** (only if you're applying to a bachelor's program with an associate degree completed).
- **Proof of Overseas Korean status** (only for Overseas Koreans sub-track).
- **Proof of Korean citizenship renunciation** (only if you previously held Korean citizenship).
- **Proof of Korean War veteran's descendant** (only if claiming the 5% bonus).
- **Passport copy** (required if proof of citizenship doesn't clearly indicate citizenship info).

**Optional but heavily rewarded:**

- **TOPIK score report** — Level 3+ adds 10% bonus points; Level 5+ exempts you from the language year.
- **TOEFL / IELTS score report** — sliding-scale bonus points.
- **Awards and certificates** — anything you reference in your personal statement or study plan should be backed by evidence.

## The apostille step (don't underestimate this)

For **round 2** (when survivor documents reach NIIED), all required certificates need to be **apostilled** if your country is part of the [Apostille Convention](https://www.hcch.net/en/instruments/conventions/specialised-sections/apostille), or **consular-confirmed** if it isn't.

What this means in practice:

- Your high school graduation certificate, transcript, family register, and any other certificates need to go through your country's apostille authority (usually the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, Ministry of External Affairs, or Department of State equivalent).
- This typically takes **2–4 weeks**, sometimes longer in countries with backlogs (we've seen 6–8 weeks in India).
- Documents in languages other than English or Korean need a **certified translation**, plus apostille on either the original or the translation.
- High school certs and transcripts can use **consular confirmation** instead of apostille if needed.
- A simple notarized photocopy is **NOT** accepted. Notarized copies of apostilled documents are also rejected, with one narrow exception (a certified true copy issued by a Korean embassy).

<Callout variant="warn" title="apostille timing matters">
You don't need apostilled documents for round 1 (the embassy or university only needs simple copies — though some require notarized copies, set by each institution). You **do** need them at NIIED in early November (Embassy Track) or mid-November (University Track). If you start the apostille process only after passing round 1, you'll likely miss the NIIED deadline. Start apostilling **before** you submit the round 1 application.
</Callout>

## Submission requirements: round 1 vs round 2

The two rounds have very different submission expectations.

**Round 1** (to embassy or university):

- Set by **each embassy or university** — some accept photocopies, some require certified translations, some accept electronic submission, some require physical originals.
- Number of copies set by the institution (typically 1 original + 3 copies).
- All forms and certificates in **English or Korean** (or with certified translations attached).

**Round 2** (to NIIED, via the embassy/university):

- **Documents to complete (Forms 1–6):** filled in English or Korean, with original handwritten signatures. Embassy Track: 1 original + 3 photocopies. University Track: 1 original.
- **Required certificates:** apostilled or consular-confirmed. Embassy Track: 1 apostilled copy + 3 simple photocopies. University Track: 1 apostilled copy.

NIIED keeps your original documents for **5+ years** and never returns them. Don't submit one-of-a-kind documents you'll need later — submit copies that have been apostilled.

## Order of documents and labelling

Submitted documents must be **stacked in the order of the Application Checklist** (which serves as the front cover) and **numbered + labelled in the top-right corner of each document**. Example: "8. High School Graduation Certificate (Consular confirmed)".

All documents must be **A4 (or letter) sized**. Smaller documents go on a blank A4 sheet; larger ones get folded down.

## Things that get applications instantly disqualified

- Name doesn't match passport (especially **middle name** discrepancies).
- Missing one of the required forms.
- Forms missing original handwritten signatures.
- Listing a department name that doesn't match the official "University Information" file.
- Embassy Track applicant listing 3 Type A universities (no Type B) or 3 different departments at one university.
- Applying to multiple tracks, multiple universities (within University Track), or multiple sub-programs.
- Notarized photocopies of certificates (instead of properly apostilled originals) at round 2.
- Missing the December 31 graduation certificate deadline if you applied as an "expected graduate."

## A practical preparation timeline

Working backward from a typical mid-October deadline, here's a sane preparation calendar:

| When | What to do |
|---|---|
| **June – July** | Confirm eligibility. Pick your track. Identify universities/departments. Take TOPIK if your test window allows. |
| **July – August** | Start drafting Forms 2 (Personal Statement) and 3 (Study Plan). Request your recommendation letter (give the recommender 4+ weeks). Order your transcripts. |
| **August – early September** | Begin apostille process for graduation certificate, transcript, family register, citizenship docs. Get certified translations if needed. |
| **Mid-September** | Watch for NIIED guidelines and your embassy/university announcement. Finalize Forms 1, 5, 6. Polish 2 and 3. |
| **Late September – October** | Submit round 1 to embassy or university by their specific deadline. Continue apostille if not finished. |
| **October – November** | Round 1 results. If you survive, ensure all apostilled docs reach the embassy/university by their NIIED forwarding deadline (Nov 1 Embassy / Nov 15 University). |
| **November – December** | Round 2 NIIED review. Embassy Track: round 3 university review. |
| **Late December** | Embassy Track applicants notify NIIED of final university choice. |
| **Early January** | Final list published. If selected, prepare visa and prepare extra apostilled grad certs for arrival. |

<Callout variant="info" title="cgrade and certificate timing">
Two specific traps for "expected graduates":
<ul>
<li>You must submit a <strong>certificate of expected graduation</strong> (with an explicit expected date or month) at application — a plain enrollment certificate without a date is not accepted.</li>
<li>Once you graduate, you must deliver the actual graduation certificate to NIIED by <strong>December 31</strong>. Missing this deadline cancels your acceptance.</li>
</ul>
</Callout>

## After you're accepted

If you're on the final list:

- Prepare **multiple copies of your apostilled graduation certificate** before flying to Korea — you'll need them for the standard admission letter, visa, and immigration office. NIIED warns scholars not to leave Korea later just to retrieve more copies.
- Notify NIIED of your final university choice (Embassy Track) by December 27.
- If you achieved TOPIK 5 or 6 newly, send the certificate to NIIED by December 27 — it exempts you from the language year.
- Apply for your D-2 student visa via the Korean embassy.
- Plan your arrival in Korea around the start of the spring semester (typically February–March) for the language year.

## Renouncing the scholarship

If you decide to withdraw after the final announcement, you submit a signed letter of scholarship renunciation to your embassy/university. Once a scholarship is officially renounced, **it cannot be reversed** — you cannot reapply for GKS in the future. Repeated renunciations from the same country can also cause that country to lose its GKS quota in future cycles.

This is rare but worth understanding before accepting. If you're going to back out, do it before the final announcement, not after.
