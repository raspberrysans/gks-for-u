The **Global Korea Scholarship (GKS)** is the South Korean government's flagship full-ride scholarship for international students. The undergraduate edition — known as **GKS-U** — covers tuition, airfare, a year of intensive Korean language training, and a monthly living stipend for the entire degree. If you are a high-school graduate (or about to be) from one of the invited countries, it is one of the most generous scholarships in Asia, and the application is open to anyone who meets the eligibility criteria.

This is a long guide because GKS-U is, intentionally, a layered program. It has six different sub-tracks, two umbrella application paths, and a multi-stage selection process that runs from late summer through early January. Most applicants get tripped up not by the academics but by misunderstanding which track they should be applying through. Read this guide once, and the rest of your application will make a lot more sense.

> The numbers, dates, and quotas below come from NIIED's most recently published guideline (the 2025 cycle). The 2026 cycle is expected to mirror this structure, but always cross-check the official PDF on [studyinkorea.go.kr](https://www.studyinkorea.go.kr) before submitting.

## What is the Global Korea Scholarship?

GKS is administered by the **National Institute for International Education (NIIED)**, an arm of the Korean Ministry of Education. The undergraduate program (GKS-U) sponsors international students who want to pursue a **bachelor's** or **associate degree** at a Korean university, plus the **one-year Korean language program** that comes before the degree.

Two important things to understand up front:

- **It is not just a tuition discount.** GKS pays tuition, gives you a stipend, covers your flight to Korea, and pays for your Korean lessons. Total value over a 5–7 year bachelor's program runs into tens of thousands of US dollars.
- **It is *the* government scholarship.** GKS replaced what used to be called "KGSP" (Korean Government Scholarship Program) in 2010. If you read about KGSP online, those guides describe the same program — just under its old name.

In the most recent cycle, NIIED awarded **367 GKS-U scholarships** worldwide, distributed across six sub-tracks. Those numbers change slightly year to year, but the structure has been stable for several years now.

## What does the GKS-U scholarship actually cover?

For every selected scholar, NIIED provides:

| Benefit | Amount (per the 2025 cycle) |
|---|---|
| Tuition | Up to **5,000,000 KRW** per semester (universities waive the rest) |
| Korean language training | **5,200,000 KRW** for four quarters at an approved KLI |
| Airfare to Korea | Economy-class one-way ticket (actual expense) |
| Monthly stipend (Korean program) | **12,800,000 KRW per year** (≈ ₩1.07M / month) |
| Monthly stipend (degree program) | **13,600,000 KRW per year** (≈ ₩1.13M / month) |
| Settlement allowance | One-time payment on arrival |
| Medical insurance | Covered |
| TOPIK exam fee, completion grant, research/printing grants | Included |

In practice, that means a GKS-U scholar studying for a four-year bachelor's degree receives roughly **65–75 million KRW in stipends and benefits** across the program, on top of fully waived tuition.

<Callout variant="info" title="topik bonus stipend">
If you reach **TOPIK Level 5 or 6** before or during your scholarship, you receive an automatic Korean Proficiency Grant on top of your monthly stipend. You can also be exempted from the language year and start your degree directly.
</Callout>

## How GKS-U is structured: the two paths

This is the part that confuses almost every first-time applicant. There are **two umbrella application paths**, and within them, **six sub-tracks**:

**Embassy Track** (87 scholars in the 2025 cycle):

- **General** — 71 scholars from 68 countries.
- **Overseas Koreans** — 7 scholars (descendants of Korean emigrants).
- **IRTS** (International Reconstruction Talent Scholarship) — 9 scholars, **Ukrainian nationals only**.

**University Track** (280 scholars in the 2025 cycle):

- **R-GKS (Regional GKS)** — 80 scholars, regional universities outside the Seoul metro.
- **UIC (University Industry Cooperation)** — 100 scholars, science/engineering majors with industry partners.
- **Associate Degree** — 100 scholars, two-year vocational degrees.

You can apply to **only one** track in a given cycle. Picking the right one is largely a function of where you want to study, what you want to study, and which sub-track gives you the best statistical odds.

## Who can apply? The headline eligibility rules

GKS-U is restrictive in a few specific ways. The non-negotiables:

- **Citizenship.** You must hold citizenship of a NIIED-invited country. **Both you and your parents must be non-Korean citizens.** Dual citizens (Korean + another) are not eligible.
- **Age.** You must be **under 25** as of March 1 of the entry year. (For the 2025 cycle, that meant born after March 1, 2000.)
- **Education.** You must have graduated, or be expected to graduate, from a high school by the program start. **You cannot already hold a bachelor's degree.** Associate-degree holders may apply to bachelor's programs.
- **No Korean schooling.** You cannot have graduated from a Korean high school or associate-degree program.
- **Grades.** Your cumulative GPA must be **≥ 80% percentile** OR a CGPA of at least **2.64/4.0, 2.80/4.3, 2.91/4.5, or 3.23/5.0**.
- **Health.** You must be in good physical and mental health to study in Korea for the full duration.
- **No prior GKS degree scholarship.** You are ineligible if you previously received a Korean government scholarship for a degree program.

A full breakdown — including how transcripts and conversion tables work — is in our [GKS-U eligibility requirements guide](/blog/gks-eligibility-requirements).

<Callout variant="warn" title="the duplicate-application trap">
You can apply to **only one** sub-track and **one** university per cycle. Embassy Track applicants who fail the first round can re-apply through the University Track in the same cycle — but only if they failed the *first* round. Once you pass round one through the embassy, that path is locked in.
</Callout>

## The selection process at a glance

GKS-U is a three-round process for Embassy Track and a two-round process for University Track. Roughly:

1. **First round** — submit your documents to the Korean embassy or directly to a university. Document review + oral interview. Embassy results announced by mid-October; university results by mid-November.
2. **Second round** — NIIED reviews documents of first-round survivors. Embassy results late November; university results mid-December.
3. **Third round (Embassy Track only)** — your three chosen universities review your application. Final result by December 20.
4. **Final announcement** — the official list of GKS-U scholars is published on the Study in Korea website in early January.

A detailed timeline with all the cutoff dates is in our [GKS application timeline and document checklist](/blog/gks-application-timeline-and-documents).

## What you submit (the documents)

Every GKS-U applicant prepares the same core packet:

- **Form 1** — Application Form
- **Form 2** — Personal Statement
- **Form 3** — Study Plan
- **Form 4** — One Letter of Recommendation
- **Form 5** — GKS Applicant Agreement
- **Form 6** — Personal Medical Assessment

Plus required certificates: proof of citizenship and family relationship, your high-school graduation certificate, your transcript, and (depending on your situation) overseas-Korean documentation, Korean citizenship renunciation, or Korean War veteran's descendant proof. TOPIK or English test scores are optional but heavily reward you on the rubric.

For round two, all your certificates need to be **apostilled** (or **consular-confirmed** if your country isn't part of the Apostille Convention). Plan on this taking weeks, not days.

## Bonus points: the easy 10–15% you can win

NIIED's evaluation rubric includes explicit additional points:

- **TOPIK Level 3 or higher** — adds **10%** of total points.
- **Direct descendant of a Korean War veteran** — adds **5%**.
- **Applying to a science/engineering department** — adds **5%**.
- **Submitting TOEFL/IELTS** — points awarded on a sliding scale (60–90% of the language category by score).

If you can take TOPIK before applying, do it. The 10% bonus is the single biggest swing factor that is fully under your control.

## The personal statement and study plan are where most applicants lose

GKS-U is competitive but not because the academics are extreme. The cutline GPA (2.64/4.0) is achievable for most students. The reason acceptance rates can sit at 5–10% per country is that **the personal statement and study plan are evaluated heavily**, and they are where weak applications get filtered out.

A strong study plan answers four questions in concrete detail:

1. Why this specific major and department?
2. Why this specific university?
3. What is your semester-by-semester plan after the language year?
4. What will you do *after* you graduate, and how does Korea fit in?

A weak study plan is generic ("I want to learn Korean culture and improve my Korean") and doesn't reference the actual department's curriculum or faculty.
