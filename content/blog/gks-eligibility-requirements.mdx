Before you spend two months preparing essays, transcripts, and apostilles, settle one question: **do you actually qualify for GKS-U?** The Global Korea Scholarship for undergraduate degrees has eight hard eligibility filters. Failing any one of them disqualifies you instantly, regardless of how strong the rest of your application is.

This guide walks through every filter from the official 2025 NIIED guideline. We've ordered them by how often each one trips up applicants, not the order they appear in the PDF.

## 1. Citizenship: you and your parents

This is the rule that catches the most people off-guard.

- **You must hold citizenship of a NIIED-invited country.** The list updates each cycle, and there are roughly 70+ eligible countries.
- **Your parents (or legal guardians) must hold citizenship from a country other than Korea.** If either parent is Korean, you are not eligible.
- **You cannot be a dual citizen with Korea.** If you hold both Korean citizenship and another nationality, you are out — unless you have already officially renounced your Korean citizenship and can submit Korean-government-issued proof of the renunciation.
- **If your citizenship changes during the selection process, you are excluded from the evaluation** (this exception does not apply to UIC track applicants).

<Callout variant="warn" title="adoptees and overseas koreans">
Ethnic Koreans living abroad who do **not** hold Korean citizenship can apply through the Overseas Koreans sub-track (Embassy Track). You'll need to submit a family register or birth certificate proving your relationship to a Korean lineal ascendant.
</Callout>

## 2. Age: under 25

You must be **under 25 years of age** as of March 1 of the entry year. For the 2025 cycle, that meant born after **March 1, 2000**. For the 2026 cycle, the cutoff shifts to **March 1, 2001**.

There are no age-limit waivers, including for compulsory military service. If you'll be 26 or older when your scholarship starts, you cannot apply through GKS-U.

## 3. Education level

GKS-U is for two distinct categories:

- **Bachelor's degree program** — for applicants who graduated, or are expected to graduate, from a high school **or** an associate-degree program.
- **Associate degree program** — for applicants who graduated, or are expected to graduate, from a high school.

The hard rules:

- **Applicants who already hold a bachelor's degree cannot apply.** (Look at GKS-G for graduate programs instead.)
- **Associate-degree holders may only apply to bachelor's programs**, not to another associate degree.
- **Expected graduates** must submit a certificate of expected graduation at application, then deliver the actual graduation certificate by **December 31 of the application year** if they pass round one. Failure to deliver = automatic cancellation.
- **Provisional graduation certificates** (transcripts that don't show a graduation date) count as "expected graduation" — not as completed graduation.

## 4. GPA: the rule with two paths

Your **cumulative GPA** from your most recent qualifying program must meet **one of two conditions**:

- **Top 20% of your class** OR a **percentile score ≥ 80%** on a 100-point scale, **OR**
- **CGPA ≥ 2.64/4.0** (or 2.80/4.3, 2.91/4.5, 3.23/5.0)

You only need to satisfy one of those, not both. If your transcript only shows percentile rank and not a CGPA, that's fine — submit it as-is. If your transcript uses a non-standard scale (some Indian, Russian, and Latin-American systems do), NIIED requires you to also submit an **officially authenticated converted transcript** in one of the recognized scales. Notarized self-conversions don't count; the conversion must come from your school or university.

| Scale | Minimum acceptable CGPA |
|---|---|
| 4.0 | 2.64 |
| 4.3 | 2.80 |
| 4.5 | 2.91 |
| 5.0 | 3.23 |
| 100-point | 80 (or top 20% of class) |

<Callout variant="info" title="if your school doesn't compute a cgpa">
Some high schools issue transcripts without a CGPA. In that case, NIIED still requires either a class rank that places you in the top 20% or an officially issued document from your school explaining its grading system and confirming your standing. Submitting a transcript without either is a fast disqualification.
</Callout>

## 5. Disqualifying restrictions (read these carefully)

Even if you pass the four headline filters above, any of the following will disqualify you:

- **You graduated from a Korean high school or Korean associate-degree program.** This includes international schools located in Korea and online curricula arranged by Korean institutions. If you're in your final year at a Korean high school or university right now, you cannot apply.
- **You previously received a Korean government scholarship for a degree program.** This covers former GKS scholars (degree program), GKS scholars whose scholarship was cancelled after enrollment, and degree-program scholarship recipients from any other Korean government agency.
- **GKS Non-degree exchange students CAN apply.** This is the one positive exception — non-degree program scholars are not blocked.
- **You withdrew from or were disqualified from GKS in the last three years.** A three-year cooling-off period applies.
- **Disqualification grounds for overseas travel** — for example, certain criminal histories — make you ineligible.

## 6. The "one application" rule

GKS-U lets you apply through **exactly one track and one program per cycle**:

- **Embassy Track applicants** must pick one of: General, Overseas Koreans, or IRTS.
- **University Track applicants** must pick one of: UIC, R-GKS, or Associate Degree, **and one university and one department**.

Submitting to multiple tracks, multiple universities (within University Track), or multiple departments at the same university is automatic disqualification — and the scholarship will be cancelled retroactively if it's discovered later.

There is one nuance: **Embassy Track applicants who fail the *first* round** (document review or interview at the embassy) **may re-apply through a University Track in the same cycle**, except for IRTS applicants. Once you've passed the first round through the embassy, you're locked in.

## 7. Health

You must be in good physical and mental health to study in Korea for the full duration of the scholarship. You'll submit a **Personal Medical Assessment (Form 6)** with your application. Pre-existing conditions are not automatic disqualifiers, but conditions that would prevent you from completing 5–7 years of full-time study are.

## 8. University-specific rules (the hidden filter)

This is the rule the official guideline puts last and most applicants ignore:

> *Applicants must check additional restrictions or eligibility set by each degree university.*

In practice, this means:

- **All scholars need TOPIK Level 3 to start their degree program.** Some specific departments require **TOPIK Level 4 or higher**. If you don't reach that level by the end of the language year, the scholarship is cancelled.
- Some departments only accept new students in **March** (spring semester). If your language program ends in August and your department doesn't take September starters, you may have to wait six months and your scholarship may be affected.

Each university publishes a "University Information" file alongside the GKS notice — this file lists the available departments and their additional requirements. Read it before you commit to a university choice.

<Callout variant="warn" title="the topik level 3 trap">
Reaching TOPIK Level 3 in one year of full-time language study is achievable but not guaranteed. Roughly 20–30% of GKS scholars need to extend their language study by a semester (at their own cost). If you have any way to start studying Korean now — even Duolingo plus YouTube grammar — start.
</Callout>

## Bonus points worth chasing before you apply

These don't affect eligibility, but they directly affect your score on round one:

- **TOPIK Level 3+** — adds 10% to your total points (the single biggest controllable swing factor).
- **TOEFL or IELTS scores** — tiered points: TOEFL iBT 114+ / IELTS 8.0+ gets you 90% of the language category; lower scores still earn 60–80%.
- **Korean War veteran's descendant** — adds 5% (requires government-issued proof of lineal ancestry).
- **Science or engineering department** — adds 5% automatically based on your chosen major.

If you have a TOEFL or IELTS score sitting around from a previous test, dig it out. The score doesn't have to be tied to GKS — any valid certificate within the validity window works.

## Putting it together: the eligibility checklist

Before you start the application, confirm all of the following:

- [ ] I hold citizenship of a GKS-invited country.
- [ ] Neither of my parents holds Korean citizenship.
- [ ] I will be under 25 on March 1 of the entry year.
- [ ] I have graduated, or will graduate, from a high school by program start.
- [ ] I do not already hold a bachelor's degree.
- [ ] I did not graduate from a Korean high school or Korean associate-degree program.
- [ ] My CGPA is ≥ 2.64/4.0 (or equivalent) **OR** I'm in the top 20% of my class.
- [ ] I have never received a Korean government scholarship for a degree program.
- [ ] I am in good physical and mental health.
- [ ] I am applying to one track only.

If every box is checked, you're eligible. The next decision is which track to apply through — that's covered in our [Embassy Track vs University Track decision guide](/blog/embassy-track-vs-university-track).
